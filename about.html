About the Scholar

One person, two crafts: the quill that writes the evidence, and the query that finds it.

Most research careers divide the labor: the people who gather the data, the people who analyze it, and the people who write it up rarely share an office, let alone a desk. Mine did not divide. For nearly two decades I have been the architect of a longitudinal orthopedic outcomes registry, the analyst who queries it, and the writer who carries its findings into the peer-reviewed literature.

The registry came first. Built case by case from operative notes, radiographs, laboratory values, and patient-reported outcomes, it grew into a database of more than 7,700 hip arthroplasty cases with follow-up reaching nineteen years. A registry of that age is less a spreadsheet than a correspondence; thousands of patients writing back, year after year, so that a question asked in 2026 can be answered with data promised in 2007.

The writing followed. Seventeen-plus peer-reviewed publications, conference presentations, and regulatory documents, including work conducted under FDA IDE and PMA oversight, where precision is not a style preference but a legal requirement. I write the way I build databases: so the structure holds when someone leans on it.

The systems hold it all together. SQL, careful data dictionaries, and automated workflows turn retrospective chaos into answerable questions. It is quiet work, and it is the reason the loud work, the survivorship curves and the published papers, can be trusted.

The Quill & the Query is the working name for that whole practice: evidence, carefully kept and clearly told.
